ALBUMIN in COBAS 501

  1. PURPOSE
    1. This procedure describes the quantitative determination of albumin in human serum and plasma. (Test name on analyzer: ALB2).
    2. Hyperalbuminemia is of little diagnostic significance except in the case of dehydration. Hypoalbuminemia occurs during many illnesses and is caused by several factors: compromised synthesis due either to liver disease or as a consequence of reduced protein uptake; elevated catabolism due to tissue damage (severe burns) or inflammation; malabsorption of amino acids (Crohn’s disease); proteinuria as a consequence of nephrotic syndrome; protein loss via the stool (neoplastic disease). In severe cases of hypoalbuminemia, the maximum albumin concentration of plasma is 2.5 g/dL (380 µmol/L). Due to the low osmotic pressure of the plasma, water permeates through blood capillaries into tissue (edema).
    3. The determination of albumin allows monitoring of a controlled patient dietary supplementation and serves also as an excellent test of liver function.
  2. PRINCIPLE
    1. Method used: Colorimetric Assay
    2. At a pH of 4.1, albumin displays a sufficiently cationic character to be able to bind with bromcresol green (BCG), an anionic dye, to form a blue-green complex. The color intensity of the blue-green color is directly proportional to the albumin concentration in the sample and is measured photometrically.

      4. Calibrator: C.F.A.S.
        1. Preparation
          1. To prepare the calibrator, carefully dissolve the contents by adding exactly 3.0 mL of deionized water.
          2. Dissolve the contents completely within 30 minutes by occasional gentle swirling. Avoid the formation of foam.
          3. Transfer 200 µL aliquots of each reconstituted calibrator into empty aliquot containers (e.g. COBAS 6000 sample cups) labeled with the calibrator name and the date reconstituted. Seal each tube with parafilm.
        2. Storage
          1. The unopened calibrator vials are stored in the medical refrigerator at 2-8ºC and are stable until the expiration date indicated on the bottle.
          2. Store the aliquoted calibrators at -20°C, protected from light. A label indicating the calibrator name, date of reconstitution, lot number, expiry date after opening/reconstitution, and initials of the person who prepared the calibrators, must accompany each batch of reconstituted calibrators.
        3. Handling and stability
          1. Ensure that aliquots are thawed to room temperature and properly mixed before use.
          2. Stability of the components in the reconstituted calibrator
            1. 8 hours at 15-25 °C
            2. 2 days at 2-8 °C
            3. 4 weeks at (-25)-(-15) °C(when frozen once)

  20. LIMITATIONS
    1. Icterus: No significant interference up to an I index of 60 for conjugated and unconjugated bilirubin (approximate conjugated and unconjugated bilirubin concentration: 1026 μmol/L or 60 mg/dL).
    2. Hemolysis: No significant interference up to an H index of 1000 (approximate hemoglobin concentration: 621 µmol/L or 1000 mg/dL)
    3. Lipemia (Intralipid): No significant interference up to an L index of 550. There is poor correlation between the L index (corresponds to turbidity) and triglycerides concentration.
    4. Drugs: No interference was found at therapeutic concentrations using common drug panels. In very rare cases, gammopathy, in particular type IgM (Waldenström’s macroglobulinemia), may cause unreliable results.
    5. Colorimetric methods used for the determination of Albumin may lead to falsely elevated test results in patients suffering from renal failure or insufficiency due to interference with other proteins. Immunoturbidimetric methods are less affected.
  21. CALCUALTIONS
    1. Conversion factors
      1. g/L x 15.2 = µmol/L
      2. µmol/L x 0.0658 = g/L
      3. g/L x 0.1 = g/dL
